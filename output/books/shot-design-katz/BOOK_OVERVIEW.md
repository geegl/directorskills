# BOOK_OVERVIEW — 大师镜头 (Master Shots)
**Author:** Christopher Kenworthy (克里斯托弗·肯沃斯)
**Translator:** 姚钎
**Source:** 104 pages, 12 chapters, ~100 techniques
**Slug:** shot-design-katz (mapped to master-shots-v1 content)

---

## 1. One-Sentence Summary
A practitioner's compendium of 100+ camera movement and framing techniques for narrative filmmaking, organized by dramatic situation (fight, chase, dialogue, horror, romance), each showing how to create specific emotional effects through lens choice, camera positioning, and choreographed movement.

## 2. Core Thesis
**"拍电影就像编舞"** — Filmmaking is choreography. Every shot requires designing the dance between actor and camera. Mastery of technique liberates creativity; you must learn the rules deeply enough to forget them.

## 4. Author's Framework: The Five Variables
Every technique manipulates these five variables:
1. **镜头焦距 (Focal Length)** — Wide-angle exaggerates motion/distance; telephoto compresses space
2. **摄影机位置 (Camera Position)** — Height, distance, angle relative to subject
3. **摄影机运动 (Camera Movement)** — Push, pull, pan, track, orbit
4. **演员编排 (Actor Choreography)** — Direction, speed, blocking
5. **时间节奏 (Timing)** — When to move, when to hold, when to cut

## 5. Recurring Principles
- **P1: Telephoto Compression** — Long lenses shorten perceived distance (punch illusion, chase tension, intimacy)
- **P2: Wide-angle Exaggeration** — Short lenses amplify motion speed and spatial depth
- **P3: Eye-Position Anchoring** — Keep actor's eye at same screen position during push-in to hide camera movement
- **P4: Camera-as-Psychology** — Camera movement mirrors audience emotion (retreat = fear, push = curiosity)
- **P5: Misdirection Before Shock** — Redirect attention before surprise; the setup matters more than the scare
- **P6: Continuity Through Movement** — Use actor motion to justify camera repositioning (no cuts needed)
- **P7: Static = Power** — Stillness creates tension, importance, or dread

## 6. Target Reader
- Independent filmmakers with basic equipment (handheld camera, tripod, basic lenses)
- Directors who want practical shot designs, not theory
- People transitioning from amateur to professional shot planning

## 7. Strengths
- Extremely practical: every technique has step-by-step instructions
- Organized by dramatic situation (not by technical category) — you look up "how to shoot a chase" not "how to use a telephoto"
- Rich film references (Fight Club, Amelie, Blade Runner, The Shining, etc.)
- Acknowledges budget constraints — most techniques work with minimal equipment

## 8. Limitations
- Chinese translation quality varies; some OCR artifacts present
- Techniques are presented in isolation; less guidance on combining them into sequences
- No coverage of digital/post-production workflows
- Focused on narrative fiction; limited documentary/applicability

## 9. Adler Classification
- **Practical Book** — "How to" do something
- **Level: Inspectional** — Can be skimmed chapter-by-chapter; each section is self-contained
- **Organizing Question:** "How do I make this specific dramatic moment look professional on camera?"
