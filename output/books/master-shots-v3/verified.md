## V1: Lens-Emotion Mapping (镜头-情绪映射框架)

**Source**: Chapter 1 (1.1–1.10)
**Type**: Framework
**Slug**: `msv3-lens-emotion-mapping`

### Core Claim
A systematic framework mapping focal length choices to emotional effects. Long telephoto isolates and creates intimacy; wide-angle exaggerates space and creates urgency; medium focal length is neutral and observational. Movement-lens combinations create compound effects: telephoto+dolly = amplified isolation; wide-angle+tracking = exaggerated speed.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **1.4**: "长焦镜头能够让背景虚化变得更容易，让演员与画面相分离。这种效果非常适合于戏剧化瞬间或揭示场面"
- **1.6**: "广角镜头夸大了距离，因此摄影机运动显得更迅速"
- **1.7**: Fixed wide-angle with actor choreography creates 7 compositions in one take
- **2.2**: Camera speed matching + foreground objects = speed perception
- **7.7**: Near-180° angle + low camera = speed without fast cutting

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "I want a scene that feels both intimate and urgent — what lens do I use?" → A: Telephoto for intimacy + fast dolly approach. The tension between isolation and approach creates the dual effect.
- Q: "My wide-angle close-up makes the actor's nose look huge — how do I fix it?" → A: Shoot from slightly above (1.6 mitigation technique).

### V3 Uniqueness
The specific mapping of focal length → emotion → movement combination is non-obvious. While "wide angle = distortion" is basic, the systematic framework of lens+movement compound effects and their emotional mapping is genuinely specialized knowledge.

## V2: Symbolic Staging (象征性场景调度框架)

**Source**: Chapter 5 (5.1–5.8)
**Type**: Framework
**Slug**: `msv3-symbolic-staging`

### Core Claim
Encode narrative meaning through camera position and movement rather than dialogue. Double push = attraction; camera proximity shift = power exchange; depth of field interruption = intimacy disruption; actor repositioning = group dissolution.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **5.1**: "每个镜头都应该揭示演员、传达故事，但技术运用最熟练的导演会力图在机位设置中传达象征性意义"
- **5.1** (Heavenly Creatures): Double push-in toward both actors visualizes mutual attraction
- **5.5**: Camera proximity to actor = audience identification with that actor
- **5.3**: Focus pull between actors = intimacy interruption metaphor
- **5.8**: Actor repositioning in multi-person scene = group breaking apart

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "How do I show two characters are attracted to each other without any dialogue about it?" → A: Use alternating push-ins toward both (5.1 double push technique).
- Q: "How do I show a group falling apart?" → A: Start with tight grouping, then have actors physically reposition to create individual frames (5.8).

### V3 Uniqueness
The specific camera-as-metaphor techniques (double push = attraction, camera "theft" = power takeover) are not common knowledge. This is specialized symbolic vocabulary for visual storytelling.

## V3: Power-Distance Framework (权力-距离框架)

**Source**: 5.5, 5.4, 9.10
**Type**: Framework
**Slug**: `msv3-power-distance`

### Core Claim
Whoever is closer to the camera holds perceived power. When one actor approaches the camera while the dolly retreats, the approaching actor "takes over" the scene. Camera height modulates: low angle = power, high angle = vulnerability (unless actor doesn't look up). Seated actors with camera above = powerlessness despite position.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **5.5**: "谁离摄影机更近，我们就会认为谁控制着状况" + "摄影机放置的位置对于表现场景里谁掌控着权力平衡有着巨大的影响"
- **5.5** (Book of Eli): Oldman approaches camera, dolly retreats, after cut camera stays near Oldman — he has "taken" the camera from Washington
- **5.4** (Leon): High angle + forward movement + no upward gaze = overwhelming presence
- **9.10** (Love Actually): Camera above seated PM makes him look nervous despite his authority

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "I want to show a power shift mid-scene without dialogue — how?" → A: Have the gaining actor physically approach the camera while the losing actor recedes, then cut to maintain proximity to the new power holder.
- Q: "How do I make a seated authority figure look vulnerable?" → A: Camera above, looking down, while they look up at the other character (9.10).

### V3 Uniqueness
The specific mechanic of "camera theft" — one actor physically taking the camera's proximity from another — is a genuinely non-obvious technique. The insight that camera proximity = audience identification = perceived control is specialized film grammar.

## V5: Speed Conveyance Framework (速度传达框架)

**Source**: 7.7, 2.2, 7.1, 7.6
**Type**: Framework
**Slug**: `msv3-speed-conveyance`

### Core Claim
Convey speed without fast cutting through layered techniques: near-180° camera angle matching actor path, low angle for ground proximity, wide-angle lens for peripheral distortion, foreground objects creating motion blur, distance variation during orbit. Combining two different motion types in one shot is more impactful than cutting between them.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **7.7**: "以几乎相对的角度进行拍摄，一个短镜头能比一堆快速剪辑创造出更多的速度感"
- **2.2**: "让摄影机和演员之间出现物体。当这些物体闪过，形成虚化，效果便能得到强化"
- **7.1**: Combining two different motion types (actor path + camera stop) in one shot
- **7.6**: Distance variation during orbit + passing behind objects = perceived speed increase

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "My chase scene feels slow despite fast editing — what's wrong?" → A: Use near-perpendicular camera angle (7.7), add foreground objects (2.2), lower camera angle for ground proximity.
- Q: "How do I make a training montage feel intense without cutting fast?" → A: Orbit with distance variation, pass behind columns (7.6).

### V3 Uniqueness
The near-180° angle technique (shooting almost perpendicular to actor's path) is genuinely non-obvious. The layered approach combining angle, height, foreground objects, and lens choice as a systematic speed framework is specialized knowledge.

## P1: Body Performance Over Face (肢体表演优先原则)

**Source**: 8.2, 2.7
**Type**: Principle
**Slug**: `msv3-body-performance`

### Core Claim
In scenes of emotional tension, body language and posture are as important as — sometimes more important than — facial expressions. Use wide/medium shots to capture physical acting. Tell actors you're shooting this way so they adjust performance accordingly. Some actors try to keep their face on screen at all times; this ruins body-language scenes.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **8.2**: "一般的导演可能倾向于使用特写，但身体动作在演员内心挣扎着是否要交流和接触的场景里更加重要"
- **2.7**: "有些演员希望他们的脸能一直出现在屏幕上。如果你的演员尝试那样做，很可能会毁掉这一类场景"
- **8.2** (Leon): Natalie Portman's lounging posture communicates emotional state more than her face could

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "My actor insists on close-ups for an emotional scene — should I comply?" → A: No. If the scene involves hesitation or physical tension, wider shots capturing body language will be more effective. Tell the actor you're shooting wide so they adjust their physical performance.
- Q: "How do I direct actors for a body-language-heavy scene?" → A: Communicate the shot choice upfront so they invest in physical performance rather than facial micro-expressions.

### V3 Uniqueness
Counter-intuitive: most directors (and actors) default to close-ups for emotion. The specific insight that body language in wide shots can outperform facial close-ups for certain emotional states, and that actors need to be told the shot choice to adjust, is genuinely non-obvious.

## P7: Unexpected Reveal Principle (意外揭示原则)

**Source**: 3.4, 3.5, 4.6
**Type**: Principle
**Slug**: `msv3-unexpected-reveal`

### Core Claim
Break audience expectations by having actors enter their own POV shots, or by revealing unexpected subjects through camera movement. When an actor walks into what the audience assumed was their POV, it creates unease, surprise, and destroys the voyeuristic identification that was just established.

### V1 Cross-Domain Evidence
- **3.4** (An Education): "让人意外的是，穆里根随后从左侧入画，走到了场景中。实质上，演员走进了她自己的视点里"
- **3.5** (Harry Potter): Camera pull-back reveals unexpected actor at the new position
- **4.6** (Leon): Pan-tilt reveals the full scene the actor was gesturing toward

### V2 Predictive Power
- Q: "I want to create a subtle sense of unease in a scene where a character is being secretive — how?" → A: Establish what seems to be their POV, then have them walk into the shot, destroying the voyeuristic connection.
- Q: "How do I make a simple reveal feel surprising?" → A: Let the audience assume the camera movement is for one purpose, then reveal an unexpected subject.

### V3 Uniqueness
The specific technique of an actor entering their own POV shot is genuinely non-obvious and counter-intuitive. Most filmmakers use POV shots as a one-way street; the idea of breaking that contract to create psychological disruption is specialized knowledge.
