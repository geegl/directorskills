# Body Performance Over Face (肢体表演优先原则)

## R — Reading

> "一般的导演可能倾向于使用特写，但身体动作在演员内心挣扎着是否要交流和接触的场景里更加重要。" — 8.2

> "有些演员希望他们的脸能一直出现在屏幕上。如果你的演员尝试那样做，很可能会毁掉这一类场景。" — 2.7

## I — Interpretation

In scenes of emotional tension — especially those involving hesitation, internal conflict, or uncertain intimacy — **body language and posture are as important as, sometimes more important than, facial expressions**. This is counter-intuitive: most directors (and actors) default to close-ups for emotional scenes.

The principle has two dimensions:

1. **Shot Selection**: Use wider shots (medium, medium-wide) to capture the full body. A character's slouch, lean, turn-away, or hesitant reach communicates emotional state that a close-up would miss (8.2, Leon — Natalie Portman's lounging posture).

2. **Actor Communication**: Tell actors *before shooting* that you're using wider shots. This is critical because:
   - Actors who know they're in close-up invest in facial micro-expressions
   - Actors who know they're in wide shots invest in physical performance
   - If an actor tries to keep their face prominent in a body-language scene, it undermines the technique (2.7)

The underlying logic: some emotional states are *physical* — the tension of wanting to approach but holding back, the discomfort of an intimate situation, the attempt to appear casual while hiding distress. These states manifest in the body, not the face.

## A1 — Past Application

| Film | Technique | Effect |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 《这个杀手不太冷》(Leon) | Wide/medium shots of Portman lounging (8.2) | Her posture communicates emotional state more than her face could |
| 《月桂谷》(Laurel Canyon) | Lateral tracking with body language focus (2.7) | Beckinsale's pursuit of Bale reads through body positioning, not facial close-ups |
| 《这个杀手不太冷》(Leon) | Over-shoulder from Portman's height, Reno's body in frame (8.2) | Reno's discomfort visible through posture, not expression |

## A2 — Future Trigger

**Trigger scenarios:**
- "My actor insists on close-ups for an emotional scene" → If the scene involves hesitation, physical tension, or uncertain intimacy, wider shots will be more effective. Communicate the shot choice to the actor.
- "The scene feels emotionally flat even though the acting is good" → Consider whether the emotion is physical rather than facial. Switch to wider shots.
- "How do I direct actors for a body-language-heavy scene?" → Tell them upfront you're shooting wide so they invest in physical performance.
- "Two characters are circling each other emotionally" → Use medium shots that capture their full body positioning.

**Language signals:**
- "the emotion isn't landing," "feels flat despite good acting"
- "they're hesitating," "internal conflict," "wanting to approach but holding back"
- "actor wants more close-ups"
- "body language," "posture," "physical tension"

## E — Execution

### Step 1: Identify the Emotional Type
Ask: Is this emotion primarily *facial* (grief, shock, realization) or *physical* (hesitation, discomfort, attempted casualness)?
- Facial → Close-up appropriate
- Physical → Wider shot needed (8.2)

### Step 2: Choose the Shot
For body-language scenes:
- **Medium-wide**: Captures full torso + arms for gestural acting
- **Medium**: Good for posture + head position
- **Creative over-shoulder**: Frame the subject through the other character's body, emphasizing physical relationship (8.2)

### Step 3: Communicate with Actors
Before shooting, tell the actor:
- "I'm shooting this wide/medium, so I need your body to tell the story"
- "Your posture matters more than your expression in this shot"
- This prevents actors from unconsciously trying to "show their face" to the camera (2.7)

### Step 4: Watch for Face-Seeking Behavior
Some actors will subtly turn toward the camera or adjust their position to keep their face in frame. If this happens during a body-language scene, redirect them. The shot's power comes from the body, not the face.

## B — Boundary

### When NOT to Use
- **Close-up emotional climaxes**: Moments of realization, grief, or shock are better served by facial close-ups
- **Dialogue-heavy scenes**: If the scene is primarily about what's being said, facial expressions carry more information
- **When the actor's face IS the story**: Some scenes are specifically about reading someone's expression (poker scenes, interrogation reveals)

### Author Blind Spots
- Kenworthy's examples are all from dramatic/art-house cinema. In comedy, body language works differently (physical comedy vs. emotional tension).
- The principle assumes actors who are skilled at physical performance. Not all actors can convey emotion through body language effectively.
- No discussion of how costume design affects body-language readability — a character in a bulky coat reads differently from one in a fitted outfit.

### Counter-Example
If you use wide shots for an emotional scene but the actor is only performing with their face (because they weren't told about the shot choice), the result is a scene where the emotion is invisible. Communication with the actor is essential (2.7).
